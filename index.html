An end-to-end pipeline combining YOLO + SAM 2 tracking, ProPainter inpainting, and anti-jitter trajectory smoothing to seamlessly replace moving vehicles in aerial drone footage.

Implementation Overview

Modifying moving objects in dynamic drone footage introduces significant challenges in temporal consistency, spatial memory limits, and trajectory jitter. This page documents my implementation of a robust, VRAM-efficient initial pipeline for drone-scene object replacement utilizing state-of-the-art computer vision models.

I engineered an auto-exposure stabilized tracking phase leveraging a finetuned top-down YOLO model paired with SAM 2. By extracting rotated bounding boxes (via cv2.minAreaRect), I successfully captured the true physical body angle of the target vehicle, completely bypassing the limitations of standard axis-aligned bounding boxes. To ensure clean removals without drop-shadow artifacts, I utilized heavily dilated masks and processed the footage through ProPainter using split-inference chunking for consumer GPUs. Finally, I built a custom compositing engine with a 21-frame moving average filter to inject 2D assets flawlessly.

Pipeline Steps & Execution

Step 1: Tracking & Masking

First, I implemented a stabilization loop to account for the drone camera's auto-exposure shifts. I then passed the frames to a fine-tuned YOLO model to detect the target vehicle.

Once targeted, SAM 2 generated pixel-perfect segmentation masks. Instead of relying on jittery bounding boxes for directionality, I used OpenCV to wrap the SAM 2 mask in a rotated bounding box, allowing us to extract and save the true physical heading angle and center coordinates of the car to a log file.

SAM 2 Masking & Tracking

Step 2: Deep Temporal Inpainting

Because SAM 2 masks the car perfectly but leaves the drop shadow behind, I mathematically dilated the binary masks by 25 pixels to swallow the shadow entirely.

I then downscaled the footage to 360p to protect GPU memory and ran ProPainter. I forced the optical flow network to look 60 frames deep into the past and future. Because the expanded mask removed all traces of the car, the inpainter successfully hallucinated a perfectly clean, seamless road texture.

ProPainter Background Generation

Step 3: Trajectory Compositing

Finally, I built a Python compositing engine to inject the new 2D asset (a Ferrari). Raw tracking data inherently suffers from single-pixel vibrations and angular snap-glitches.

I implemented strict mathematical continuity to prevent the car from flipping 180 degrees randomly. I then unwrapped the radians and applied a heavy 21-frame moving average filter to both the position coordinates and the rotation angle, creating a Steadicam-like invisible rail for the asset to glide smoothly across the inpainted background.

Final Smoothed Compositing

Next Steps: Extending to 3D Scene Simulation

While this 2D pixel-space pipeline provides a high-fidelity baseline for asset tracking and background clean-plating, true simulation requires 3D geometric awareness. The next phase of this work involves adapting 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) to the static, inpainted backgrounds. This will transition the pipeline from 2D layer compositing to full 6-DOF scene simulation, allowing novel 3D vehicle assets to experience realistic perspective changes, depth occlusions, and environmental lighting interactions.
